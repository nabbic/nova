Where does the product sit in the deal?

This shapes everything — who pays, who controls access, and how the product goes to market.

A

Buyer-side tool

Sold to PE firms and M&A advisors. They run it against target companies during diligence. Buyer controls scope, timeline, and access requests. Seller cooperates by granting credentials.

Pros

  • Clear buyer — PE firms have budget
  • Repeat customer per fund
  • Advisor channel (Big 4, boutiques)

Cons

  • Seller may resist granting access
  • Each deal needs fresh setup
B

Neutral platform / escrow model

A trusted third-party layer. Seller connects systems once and grants controlled read-only access. The platform mediates — buyer requests data, seller approves scope, platform runs agents and delivers sanitised reports.

Pros

  • Seller comfort — they control what's shared
  • Reusable across multiple bidders
  • Strong defensibility as network grows

Cons

  • Harder sell (two parties to onboard)
  • More complex product to build
C

Seller-side readiness tool

Sold to companies anticipating a sale. They run it on themselves to produce a "technical health report" to share with buyers. Like a house inspection before listing — surfaces issues early so the seller can remediate or disclose.

Pros

  • Seller has full access — no friction
  • Used before process starts
  • Could naturally evolve into platform model

Cons

  • Buyer may distrust seller-run report
  • Narrower market (only pre-sale companies)
